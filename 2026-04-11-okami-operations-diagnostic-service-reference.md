# Operations Diagnostic Report — Service Reference

## Context
This note is a comprehensive reference document for Okami's Operations Diagnostic Report — the flagship entry-point service. It captures what the service is, how it's delivered, what the report covers section by section, pricing, and positioning philosophy. Intended as a cold-start document so any future Claude instance can understand the diagnostic end to end without needing prior conversation context.

## Summary
The Operations Diagnostic Report is Okami Consulting's primary service offering and the starting point for every client engagement. It is a structured evaluation of how a business operates — systems, processes, data flow, customer journey, team dependencies — delivered as a branded report that tells the business owner where they stand, what's blocking growth, and what to prioritize next. The diagnostic is positioned as a "let's scale your business" tool, not an AI assessment. AI may appear as one possible output of the diagnostic, but it is never the headline or the reason for the engagement. Pricing is $299, due at checkout, for a 45–60 minute consultation session that serves as the primary input for the report.

## Key Points

### Positioning
- Externally branded as "The Okami Diagnostic" (shorthand) and "Operations Diagnostic Report" (formal document title)
- Marketed as an operational scaling tool — not a pre-AI assessment
- AI is one possible recommendation output, not the framing or the hook
- The diagnostic should never be introduced in isolation — always lead with the problem (most businesses have operational gaps they can't see) and position the diagnostic as the structured answer
- Valuable to the client even if they never hire Okami for implementation — this is by design and increases trust/conversion

### Pricing & Delivery
- Price: $299, due at checkout (before the session)
- Session format: 45–60 minute consultation (primary data-gathering input)
- Deliverable: branded Operations Diagnostic Report (PDF), delivered after the session
- The session is a structured consult, not a free-form conversation — Lucas drives the session using the framework to gather inputs for each report section

### Report Structure (13 Sections)

**Section 1 — Cover + Snapshot**
Orients the reader. Includes report title, prepared for/by, date, 1–2 sentence purpose, a single-line non-technical readiness snapshot, and a brief methodology note (2–3 sentences explaining that the report evaluates operational foundations before technology). No recommendations, no metrics, no sales language.

**Section 2 — Executive Summary**
One-page compression of the entire report. Includes 3 key findings, 3 risks or constraints, a clear AI fit statement (if applicable), and 3 recommended next actions (directional only). No detail, no "how," no technical language.

**Section 3 — Readiness Scorecard**
Simple diagnostic snapshot across 4–6 readiness categories. Each category gets a simple score or tier with 1–2 sentence justification. Explicit blocker flags where applicable. No false precision, no competitor comparisons.

**Section 4 — Maturity Level Analysis (Full Page)**
The emotional anchor of the report. Describes where the business actually stands operationally — written so the owner recognizes themselves. Covers behavioral characteristics of their current stage (e.g., "decisions route through one person," "new hires learn by shadowing"), what typically breaks at this stage as the business grows, and what the next stage looks like. Connects maturity level to readiness for everything that follows in the report. No named frameworks, no stage diagrams, no recommendations yet.

**Section 5 — Data & Systems Inventory**
Documents what tools the business uses, where data lives, how information moves between systems (or doesn't), and where single points of failure or knowledge silos exist. Includes integration status, data quality flags, and dependency notes. Provides the factual foundation that the scorecard summarizes and that automation/AI sections build on. No tool recommendations, no architecture diagrams, no IT audit scope.

**Section 6 — Digital Presence & Discoverability**
Assesses whether customers can find, trust, and convert. Covers website, Google Business Profile, social presence, reviews. Identifies strengths, gaps, and impact on lead quality/volume. Not an SEO audit — only flags design issues if they affect conversion. No tool prescriptions.

**Section 7 — Customer Journey Map**
Exposes friction and revenue leakage across the full journey from discovery to repeat visit. Identifies where things slow down or break, where human effort is overused, and emotional/operational pain spots. Language stays human and non-technical. No solution design, no automation instructions.

**Section 8 — Automation Opportunities (Non-AI)**
Presents 5–8 things that could be improved without AI — wasted time, repetitive tasks, missed communications, staff overload. Framed as high/medium/low effort with clear prerequisites. No AI in this section. No tool names unless unavoidable. No implementation detail.

**Section 9 — AI Reality Check (Full Page)**
Where Okami's philosophy becomes specific to the client's business. Limitations get more space than strengths. Covers what AI is good at for this specific business, what it's bad at or dangerous for, why their constraints matter more than AI's general capability, and what would need to change before AI becomes appropriate in areas where it isn't yet. Includes prerequisite conditions for each limitation and non-negotiable human oversight guardrails. No vendors, no hype, no future promises. If something isn't ready, it says so directly.

**Section 10 — AI Use-Case Candidates**
Presents 3–5 conditional AI opportunities with scope boundaries, human-in-the-loop notes, and explicit exclusions for where AI should not be used yet. Not a proposal, not architecture, no pricing.

**Section 11 — Roadmap (0–90 Days + Optional 6–12 Months)**
Creates momentum without obligation. Broken into 0–30 / 31–60 / 61–90 day focus areas with clear dependencies. Optional long-term themes for 6–12 months. No task lists, no timelines implying commitment, no execution detail.

**Section 12 — Next Steps Checklist**
Purely client-facing. Lists decisions that need to be made, information gaps to close, and actions the business can take immediately — regardless of whether they work with Okami or anyone else. No sales language, no Okami engagement options, no assumptions of buy-in.

**Section 13 — Working Together (Closing Page)**
Separate from the diagnostic content. Brief description of how Okami works (1–2 sentences), 2–3 engagement options curated to what the report revealed (not a full service menu), and a clear next step (booking link). Neutral, confident tone. No pricing unless standardized. No urgency or scarcity tactics. One page maximum. Feels like an invitation, not a pitch.

### Framework Philosophy
- Operations before AI — the report evaluates foundations first because readiness determines outcomes
- The diagnostic is the entry point to the Okami relationship: lowest commitment, highest proof of competence
- The report itself is the portfolio piece — its quality is the marketing
- Sections 4 (Maturity Analysis) and 9 (AI Reality Check) are the most uniquely Okami sections and are given full-page treatment
- The internal writing framework (v2) includes per-section guardrails that define what each section must and must not contain, enabling consistent output and eventual delegation or AI-assisted drafting

## Decisions
**Decision**: Price the Operations Diagnostic at $299, due at checkout — **Why**: Needs to cost something to filter for serious prospects and signal professional value, while remaining accessible for small businesses.
**Decision**: Session length is 45–60 minutes — **Why**: Enough time to gather meaningful input across all framework sections without overwhelming the business owner or turning it into an open-ended consulting session.
